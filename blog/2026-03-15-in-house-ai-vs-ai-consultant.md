The question I get more than any other from B2B founders and ops leaders right now: "Should we hire someone to help us with AI, or build this capability ourselves?"

The honest answer is that most teams are asking the wrong version of this question. They're asking "which is better?" when the real question is "which is right for this specific situation?" — and the answer depends on a set of variables most people haven't mapped yet.

This post gives you a framework for making the call with data instead of instinct.

---

## Why This Decision Is Harder Than It Looks

The "build vs. buy" decision in software has a well-worn playbook. AI doesn't fit neatly into that playbook for three reasons.

**First, the technology is changing faster than hiring cycles.** A developer you hire today to build AI systems is working with skills they acquired in a landscape that looked different 12 months ago. An AI consultant who is actively working across five or ten client implementations right now has seen failure modes and solutions your in-house hire won't encounter for another year or two.

**Second, the internal skill set is genuinely rare.** There's a difference between a developer who has used AI tools and someone who knows how to design, implement, evaluate, and maintain AI systems in production. The latter is a small group and they're expensive. If your budget for the role is under €80K (or $100K in North America), you're not getting the person you think you're getting.

**Third, the cost of a failed implementation is high.** AI automation projects that fail don't just fail quietly — they consume engineering time, create organizational scar tissue around AI initiatives, and make the next attempt harder to fund and get buy-in for. The [failure rate for enterprise AI projects](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai) is high, and the failure mode is usually not "the AI couldn't do it" but "the team couldn't implement it correctly."

## The Framework: Four Variables That Determine the Answer

### Variable 1: Is This a Core Capability or a Commodity Function?

The most important question is whether AI automation is something you'll need to build deep institutional knowledge around, or whether it's a function you'll configure once and maintain minimally.

**Core capability** — you're using AI to create a product differentiator, a proprietary process, or a competitive moat. Examples: a company that uses AI to analyze its own customer data in ways competitors can't replicate; a firm building AI into the product itself; a team that will need to continuously iterate on AI systems as the business evolves.

**Commodity function** — you need to automate a defined process (lead qualification, invoice processing, scheduling, customer support triage) that doesn't require custom model development, just competent implementation.

If it's a core capability: you need in-house expertise eventually. A consultant can get you started, but the long-term answer is internalization.

If it's a commodity function: a consultant who has solved this exact problem before is faster, cheaper, and less risky than building internal capability from scratch.

### Variable 2: What's Your Timeline?

An in-house hire takes 6–12 weeks to recruit, 2–4 months to onboard, and another 3–6 months to understand your systems well enough to be genuinely productive. In the best case, you have something meaningful in production in 9 months.

A consultant with relevant experience can be operational in 2–3 weeks and delivering in 4–6.

If your timeline is less than 6 months, in-house is almost never the right answer — not because the hire can't do the work, but because they won't have time to do it well.

### Variable 3: How Specific Is the Problem?

Some AI problems are highly specific to your business — your data, your workflow, your integration requirements. These benefit from someone who will be embedded in your context over time.

Other AI problems are pattern-matched across industries. If you're a professional services firm that needs to automate lead enrichment, that's the same problem a hundred other firms have already solved. A consultant who has solved it before brings the pattern; you get the solution without the pattern-discovery cost.

**The test:** Could you clearly describe the problem to someone who knows nothing about your industry and have them solve it? If yes, it's likely a generic problem that benefits from external expertise. If no — if understanding the problem requires deep context about your customers, your data, or your workflows — an embedded relationship matters more.

### Variable 4: What Do You Want to Own vs. What Do You Want to Delegate?

This is a strategy question, not a budget question.

Some companies want to build AI competency as an organizational capability — they want their team to understand how the systems work, to be able to troubleshoot them, to iterate on them independently. If this is you, a pure consulting engagement leaves you dependent. You need knowledge transfer and internal upskilling built into the engagement, not just a working system at the end.

Other companies want to delegate the problem entirely — they want a system that works, maintained by someone who understands it. They don't want to become AI experts; they want to run their business. If this is you, ongoing managed services from a consultant (or agency) may be more aligned with your goals than an internal hire who you'll need to manage, retain, and keep current.

Neither answer is wrong. But they require different decisions.

## The Hybrid Path Most Teams Get Wrong

Many companies try to split the difference: hire a consultant to build the system, then hand it off to an internal person who wasn't part of the build.

This fails predictably. The internal person inherits a system they don't understand, can't maintain effectively, and can't evolve because they weren't part of the decisions that shaped it. The consultant's knowledge walks out the door with them.

If you're planning a handoff, build the internal person into the engagement from day one. They should be present for scoping, architecture decisions, and implementation — learning the system as it's built, not reading documentation after the fact.

A good AI consultant structures engagements explicitly for this. If yours doesn't, ask directly: "What does knowledge transfer look like in your engagement model?"

## When In-House Is Clearly Right

- You're building AI into your core product, not automating a business process
- You have a 12+ month horizon and the budget to hire at the right level (expect €80–120K for a genuinely senior AI engineer in Western Europe; $110–150K+ in North America)
- You want to develop internal IP and institutional knowledge as a competitive differentiator
- The problems you're solving are genuinely proprietary to your business and will require continuous iteration

## When a Consultant Is Clearly Right

- You need results in under 6 months
- The automation you need is well-defined and has been solved before in your industry
- You want to validate the ROI before committing to internal investment
- You don't want to manage an AI team — you want a working system
- Your internal team doesn't have the capacity to manage both their current work and an AI project simultaneously

## When the Answer Is "Both, in Sequence"

- Start with a consultant to validate the problem and build a first system
- Use the engagement to educate your internal team
- Transition maintenance and iteration in-house once the approach is proven
- Retain the consultant in an advisory capacity for major iterations

This sequence — consultant builds, team learns, team inherits — is the most common path to sustainable in-house AI capability at B2B companies with fewer than 200 employees.

## The Questions to Ask Before You Decide

Before making either hire, answer these:

1. **What specific process are we automating, and what does success look like in 90 days?** If you can't answer this clearly, neither path will work.

2. **What's our real timeline?** Be honest about when you need this live, not when you'd like it.

3. **Is this a one-time project or ongoing work?** A defined project favors a consultant. Ongoing evolution favors in-house.

4. **Who on our current team understands our data well enough to evaluate an AI solution?** If the answer is "no one," you need a consultant to get started — full stop.

5. **What's the cost of getting this wrong?** If failure is low-stakes (small internal tool), in-house experimentation is fine. If failure means missed revenue targets, customer-facing problems, or regulatory exposure, external expertise reduces the risk materially.

**Is it cheaper to build AI capabilities in-house or hire a consultant?**
It depends on scope and timeline. A senior in-house AI hire in Western Europe costs €80,000–120,000/year in salary alone, plus recruitment and onboarding overhead. A consultant engagement for a defined project often costs less in total than a year of in-house employment — and delivers faster. For ongoing, complex work core to your product, in-house becomes more cost-effective over a 2–3 year horizon.

**How do you know if an AI consultant is actually qualified?**
Ask for case studies from companies with similar technical constraints. Ask what went wrong on a past project and how they handled it — consultants who can't answer this haven't encountered real problems yet. Ask what tools they'll rule out before the project starts and why. And ask how they structure knowledge transfer.

**What's the biggest mistake companies make when building AI in-house?**
Hiring at the wrong level. Companies budget for a mid-level developer and expect senior AI expertise. Below a certain salary threshold you're getting someone who is learning on your project rather than applying proven expertise. The second mistake is underestimating time-to-productivity — expect 9–12 months before a new in-house hire is meaningfully productive on AI projects.

**Can a small business afford an AI consultant?**
Yes, for scoped projects. Most small businesses need 4–8 weeks of expert time to implement one well-defined automation, not a long-term retainer. Project-based engagements for specific automations are significantly more affordable than in-house hires. The key is having a specific, scoped problem rather than a vague "use AI more" mandate.

**What should a consultant deliver at the end of an engagement?**
Working systems in production, not decks and documentation. A good engagement ends with: a live automation you can observe, documentation your team can use to maintain it, clear handoff on monitoring and alert thresholds, and explicit guidance on what to do when things go wrong.
