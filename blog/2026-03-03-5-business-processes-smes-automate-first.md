## Introduction

The question isn't whether to automate business processes. For most SMEs, it's already happening — manually, expensively, and imperfectly.

Every hour a team member spends copying data between systems, chasing approvals, or sending the same update email for the third time this week is an automation problem that hasn't been solved yet. It costs real money, slows decisions down, and removes capacity from the work that actually requires human judgement.

The question is where to start.

Most SMEs don't automate based on a strategic framework. They automate whatever is costing them the most pain right now. This guide maps the five processes that come up most consistently — across industries, team sizes, and maturity levels — as the first automation wins.

If you're starting from scratch, this is usually the right order.

---

## Why These Five?

The automations that work first share three characteristics:

**High volume.** Processes that happen dozens or hundreds of times per month deliver more return from automation than low-frequency tasks, even if the individual instance is simple.

**Low variation.** The fewer exceptions and edge cases, the easier to automate reliably. Processes where 80% of instances follow the same path are ideal starting points.

**Clear current cost.** The best first automations have an obvious before/after: hours saved, errors eliminated, or delays removed. This matters for getting internal buy-in and justifying the investment.

## 1. Invoice and Accounts Payable Processing

**Why it's first:** Finance processes sit at the intersection of high volume, high variation cost, and high manual overhead. For most SMEs, at least one person is spending 20-40% of their time on invoice-related work that could be substantially automated.

**What typically gets automated:**

- Invoice capture (extraction of supplier, amount, due date from PDF or email)
- Three-way matching (invoice against purchase order against receipt)
- Approval routing (invoices above thresholds go to the right approver automatically)
- Payment scheduling (approved invoices queued for payment runs)
- Remittance and reconciliation updates to accounting software

**What this looks like in practice:**

A supplier sends a PDF invoice to a generic accounts@company.com address. An automation reads the invoice, extracts the key fields, matches it against the open PO in the ERP system, and routes it to the line manager who raised the PO for a one-click approval. Once approved, it enters the payment queue. No data entry. No manual routing. No "did you get my invoice?" emails.

**Tools commonly used:** Dext, Lightyear, ApprovalMax, Xero/QuickBooks integrations, or custom document extraction pipelines for higher volumes.

**Realistic outcome:** 60-70% reduction in manual finance processing time. Error rates from manual data entry typically drop to near zero.

## 2. Lead Capture and CRM Data Entry

**Why it's second:** Sales processes suffer from a specific problem: the people doing the selling are also doing the data administration, and they hate it. Every minute a sales rep spends logging calls, updating contact records, or moving deals through pipeline stages manually is time not spent selling.

More significantly: when reps skip the admin, the data quality in the CRM degrades. Within months, the CRM is no longer a reliable picture of the pipeline — it's a partial record no one fully trusts.

**What typically gets automated:**

- Lead capture from web forms → CRM contact creation
- Email and calendar sync (activity logged automatically without manual entry)
- Lead scoring based on engagement signals
- Deal stage updates triggered by activity (proposal sent → moves to "Proposal" stage automatically)
- Follow-up task creation when no response received within X days

**What this looks like in practice:**

A prospect fills in a contact form. They're created as a lead in the CRM, tagged with the campaign that drove them, assigned to the right rep based on territory or round-robin, and a personalised introduction email goes out within 5 minutes. The rep wakes up to a warm lead, with context, already in their pipeline — instead of manually processing a spreadsheet of overnight form submissions.

**Tools commonly used:** HubSpot, Salesforce with automation rules, Pipedrive workflows, Zapier/Make for CRM integration with other tools.

**Realistic outcome:** 3-5 hours per week per sales rep returned to selling. Lead response times drop from hours to minutes, which materially affects conversion rates.

## 3. Employee Onboarding Administration

**Why it's third:** Onboarding has a problem that's invisible until someone measures it. The average SME onboarding process involves the same set of 15-25 tasks every single time a new hire joins: IT equipment requests, system access provisioning, contract signing, payroll setup, tool invitations, orientation scheduling, HR form collection.

Every one of these tasks is performed manually, usually by 3-4 different people, and depends on email chains to coordinate. New hires routinely start without access to systems they need. Tasks fall through the gaps. The experience is chaotic for the hire and expensive for the business.

**What typically gets automated:**

- Conditional task assignment when a new hire is added to the HR system (IT ticket created, manager notified, payroll team alerted)
- Document collection and e-signature workflows
- System access requests (timed to start date)
- Equipment ordering and provisioning tracking
- Day 1 orientation schedule and resource pack generation
- 30-day check-in reminder to manager

**What this looks like in practice:**

An offer is accepted. The recruiter marks the hire as confirmed in the ATS. Automatically: an IT equipment request goes to IT, a Slack invite goes to the hire's personal email for day 1, a task list is assigned to the hiring manager with due dates, the HR team gets a contract generation request, and payroll gets a setup notification. Nothing falls through the gap because there is no gap — the trigger handles the coordination.

**Tools commonly used:** BambooHR, Rippling, Factorial, or lightweight workflow builders (Notion + Zapier, Monday.com workflows) for smaller teams.

**Realistic outcome:** Onboarding admin reduced from 8-12 hours of coordination per hire to under 2. New hire experience significantly improved. Near-elimination of "I still don't have access to X" on day 1.

## 4. Customer Support Triage and Routing

**Why it's fourth:** Support volume tends to be both high and predictable. Most support teams find that 60-70% of incoming queries fall into a small number of categories: password resets, order status, billing questions, how-do-I questions, feature requests. These categories repeat constantly, and the decision about how to handle them is almost always the same.

Without automation, every email or message gets individually read and manually sorted before the actual support work begins. That sorting is labour with zero value — it doesn't solve anything for the customer.

**What typically gets automated:**

- Intent classification (categorise incoming support request by type)
- Auto-routing (billing queries → billing team, technical issues → technical support)
- Auto-response for high-confidence resolved queries (password reset instructions sent automatically)
- Escalation triggers (negative sentiment, VIP customer, repeat contact within 48 hours)
- SLA tracking and manager alerts when tickets breach response time targets

**What this looks like in practice:**

A customer emails support asking why their invoice amount is different this month. The automation classifies it as a billing query, routes it to the billing team queue, and sends an immediate acknowledgement with an expected response time. The billing agent opens the ticket with context already populated: account history, recent changes, current invoice. No manual triage step. No "I'll pass this to the right person" intermediate emails.

**Tools commonly used:** Intercom, Zendesk, Freshdesk (all have automation built in), or lighter-touch solutions using Help Scout with workflow rules.

**Realistic outcome:** Triage time eliminated. First response times typically drop 40-60%. Support capacity increases without headcount addition.

## 5. Reporting and Management Information

**Why it's fifth:** This one tends to come last in the list but first in strategic importance. Most SME leadership teams are flying with limited visibility — not because the data doesn't exist, but because collecting it is a manual process that no one has time to do properly.

Weekly reports are assembled by copying numbers from multiple systems into a spreadsheet. Monthly board packs take a finance manager 2-3 days to compile. The data is two weeks old by the time it reaches a decision-maker. And because the process is painful, reporting happens less frequently than it should.

**What typically gets automated:**

- Data pulls from CRM, accounting, operations tools into a central location (data warehouse or reporting database)
- Dashboard refresh (current metrics always visible without manual update)
- Scheduled report distribution (weekly trading summary sent to the leadership team automatically every Monday morning)
- Anomaly alerts ("Revenue for the week is 30% below plan — flag for review")
- Board pack templating with live data integration

**What this looks like in practice:**

Every Monday at 7am, the leadership team receives an automated email with the previous week's trading summary: revenue vs plan, pipeline coverage, headcount, customer count, and 3 flagged items requiring attention. The numbers came directly from the source systems overnight. No one spent Sunday evening pulling them together.

**Tools commonly used:** Metabase, Looker Studio, Tableau (for larger data), or simpler combinations of Google Sheets + Data Studio + scheduled refreshes. For more sophisticated reporting, dbt + a warehouse like BigQuery or Snowflake.

**Realistic outcome:** 80-90% reduction in report preparation time. Leadership decisions based on current data rather than lagged snapshots. Finance and ops time freed for analysis rather than compilation.

## What These Five Have in Common

Each of these five processes shares a pattern:

- **High repetition:** The same steps, week after week
- **Multi-party coordination:** Multiple people need to pass information or take action
- **Clear rule-based logic:** If X happens, Y should happen next — consistent enough to codify
- **Measurable current cost:** Hours spent, errors made, delays caused

If you're assessing automation candidates in your own business, these are the criteria to apply. Processes that fit all four are your best starting points. Processes that don't (high variation, rare events, heavy judgement calls) are better left to humans — at least for now.

---

## Where Most SMEs Go Wrong

**Trying to automate everything at once.** Automation projects fail at the scope stage. Start with one process, demonstrate the win clearly, build internal confidence, then expand.

**Automating broken processes.** Automation scales what's already happening. If the underlying process is poorly designed, automation makes the bad outcome happen faster. Fix the process logic before codifying it.

**Underestimating the exceptions.** Every process has edge cases. The automation handles the 80% — someone still needs to handle the 20%. Build exception handling before going live.

**Choosing tools based on features rather than integration.** The most powerful automation tool that doesn't integrate with your existing systems will create more work than it saves. Integration compatibility is the first criterion, not the feature list.

**What should a small business automate first?**  
The five most common first automations are: invoice processing, lead capture and CRM data entry, employee onboarding, customer support triage, and management reporting. These all share high volume, low variation, and a clear current cost.

**How much does it cost?**  
Off-the-shelf tools (Zapier, HubSpot, BambooHR) typically cost £50-500/month and handle most of these without custom development. Custom automation projects typically run £5,000-30,000 depending on complexity.

**What's the biggest mistake?**  
Automating a broken process. Fix the workflow logic first — automation scales what's already happening, good or bad.

**How long does it take?**  
Simple workflow automations can be live in days. Multi-system integrations typically take 4-12 weeks. Full multi-process projects typically run 2-4 months.

**Can SMEs automate without technical staff?**  
Yes, for most common processes. Tools like Zapier and Make are built for non-technical users. More complex integrations benefit from technical support.
