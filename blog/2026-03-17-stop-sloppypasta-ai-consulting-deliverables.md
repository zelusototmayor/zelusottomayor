There's a term circulating in developer and product circles right now: sloppypasta. It refers to raw AI-generated content copy-pasted into work artifacts without meaningful human review — Jira tickets bloated with ChatGPT output that engineers can't implement, spec documents that are internally inconsistent, project proposals that sound authoritative but collapse under a single follow-up question.

The term went viral on Hacker News in March 2026 with 600 upvotes because it named something people had been experiencing without having words for. Teams weren't getting better deliverables because someone used AI. They were getting more of them — more text, more slides, more documentation — and the signal-to-noise ratio had gotten worse, not better.

In AI consulting, this problem is especially pronounced. The people hired to help businesses implement AI are often the worst offenders.

## What Sloppypasta Looks Like in Practice

The pattern is predictable. A consultant is hired to assess an automation opportunity, build an integration, or develop an AI strategy. They use AI tools heavily — which is fine and expected. The problem is what happens next.

Instead of distilling the AI output into something the client can act on, they forward it. A 47-page strategy document that ChatGPT drafted with minimal editing. A technical specification that Anthropic's API could have generated word-for-word. An ROI analysis full of generic industry benchmarks that have no relationship to the client's actual situation.

The client can tell something is off. The document sounds authoritative. It uses all the right terms. But when they try to do something with it — make a decision, assign tasks, prioritize — it doesn't hold up. There's no real thinking in it.

The HN thread that coined the term captured the cognitive mechanism precisely: the person producing sloppypasta felt like they learned a lot generating it, then forgot it immediately because they never actually engaged with the content. The AI did the thinking; the human just watched.

This is the consulting version of that dynamic. The deliverable exists. The client's questions remain unanswered.

## Why This Is Happening Now

AI tools have made it extraordinarily easy to produce large volumes of plausible-looking output. A consultant who would have taken two weeks to write a strategic assessment can now produce something equivalent in length and polish in two hours — if they're willing to skip the part where they actually think about it.

The pressure to do this is real. Clients expect fast turnarounds. Competitors are delivering more output faster. The temptation to let the AI do the heavy lifting and focus on the presentation layer is understandable.

But there's a meaningful difference between using AI to accelerate legitimate thinking and using AI to simulate thinking you never did.

The first looks like this: a consultant uses AI to synthesize background research, generate a first draft of a framework, or quickly model out a few scenarios — then spends serious time reviewing, correcting, pushing back on the AI's assumptions, and making sure the output actually addresses the client's specific situation. The AI saves hours on the mechanical parts. The thinking is still the consultant's.

The second looks like this: the consultant prompts the AI with the client's context, reviews the output for obvious errors, reformats it slightly, and sends it as the deliverable. The document is longer and more polished than what the consultant could have written themselves. It is also, in the ways that matter most, empty.

## The Trust Problem This Creates

The thing about sloppypasta is that it's often not detectable on first read. A well-formatted 30-page AI output can look like substantial work. The holes don't appear until someone tries to use it.

And when they do appear, the trust damage is immediate and lasting. Clients who feel like they paid for thinking and received text are unlikely to give a second chance. More importantly, the recommendations that could have actually helped them — the specific, contextual, hard-won insights that come from a consultant who genuinely understood their situation — were never produced.

This is the real cost. Not the wasted consulting fees, though those matter. The real cost is the decisions that didn't get made well because the thinking behind them was borrowed from a model that had no stake in the outcome.

## What Genuine AI-Assisted Consulting Looks Like

The differentiation in consulting right now isn't between firms that use AI and firms that don't. Almost everyone uses AI. The differentiation is between consultants who use AI to do real work faster and consultants who use AI to simulate work they're not doing.

Here's what the first category actually looks like in practice.

**The AI does research and synthesis. The consultant does judgment.** AI is extremely good at gathering and organizing information. It can pull together what's known about an industry, surface relevant patterns from a body of work, or generate a set of possible approaches to a problem. But deciding which of those approaches is right for a specific client, in a specific context, with specific constraints and stakeholders and history — that requires judgment that AI doesn't have. A consultant who's genuinely doing their job uses AI for the former and reserves the latter for themselves.

**Deliverables are short and answerable.** Genuine thinking produces concise, specific outputs that can be interrogated. A one-page strategic recommendation with clear reasoning beats a 30-page AI document every time — because the one-pager forces the author to have actually figured out what they think. If you can't fit the core recommendation on one page, you probably don't know what it is yet.

**The consultant can be challenged on it.** Sloppypasta falls apart under questioning because the author never actually understood it. Genuine work holds up because the person who produced it worked through the objections before you asked them. A consultant who reviewed every word of their own deliverable can answer follow-up questions. One who forwarded AI output can't.

**The AI tools are disclosed.** There's nothing wrong with telling a client that you used AI for certain parts of the work — specifically which parts, and what human review process those parts went through. Most sophisticated clients in 2026 assume AI is being used. What they want to know is whether a human with real skin in the game actually reviewed and owns the output.

## The Actual Differentiator

In a market where AI has made volume cheap, the differentiation is in what volume doesn't buy: genuine understanding of the client's situation, honest assessment of what will and won't work, and ownership of the recommendations.

Those things require a consultant who actually engaged with the problem — not just a consultant who can produce impressive-looking documentation about it.

This is not a pitch for slow work or against AI tools. The best consultants use AI aggressively and deliver faster than they could without it. But they use it as a tool for doing real work faster, not as a substitute for doing real work.

The clients who figure out the difference — and more of them are figuring it out every month — are the ones worth working with. And the consultants who offer the real version aren't competing with the sloppypasta producers. They're operating in a different market.

## What to Look for When Hiring an AI Consultant

If you're evaluating AI consultants and you want to avoid paying for sloppypasta, here are the questions that reveal the difference quickly.

**Ask them to walk you through a specific recommendation they've made.** Not to describe their methodology — to explain the actual reasoning behind a specific recommendation for a real client situation. Someone who genuinely did the thinking can walk you through it. Someone who forwarded AI output can't reconstruct the reasoning because they never built it.

**Ask what they disagreed with in their own analysis.** Every real strategic assessment involves tradeoffs that could have gone either way, and conclusions that the evidence supports but doesn't prove. A consultant who thought carefully about your situation will have genuine uncertainty about something. One who produced sloppypasta will express uniform confidence.

**Ask how long the deliverable took versus how long the thinking took.** The honest answer from a good consultant is that the thinking took most of the time and the deliverable was relatively fast to produce once the thinking was done. The sloppypasta version is the opposite.

**Ask who reviewed it and what they changed.** Real work gets pushed back on, refined, and edited. If no one challenged the analysis, it probably wasn't challenged because no one engaged with it deeply enough to have genuine objections.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is "sloppypasta" in the context of AI consulting?**

Sloppypasta refers to raw AI-generated content copy-pasted into client deliverables without meaningful human review or editing. The term gained traction in developer communities in 2026 to describe work that looks thorough on the surface but lacks genuine thinking — documents that are long and polished but can't stand up to follow-up questions because the person who produced them never actually engaged with the content.

**How do I know if my AI consultant is using AI responsibly?**

The clearest signal is whether they can be challenged on their own deliverables. Ask them to walk you through the reasoning behind a specific recommendation. A consultant who genuinely did the thinking will be able to explain it, acknowledge the tradeoffs, and engage with objections. One who forwarded AI output will struggle because the reasoning was never theirs.

**Isn't it fine for consultants to use AI tools? Why does it matter how they use them?**

Using AI tools is fine — expected, even. The issue is whether the AI is being used to accelerate genuine thinking or to simulate thinking that never happened. The difference shows up in whether the deliverable actually helps you make better decisions, or just gives you more text to read. The quality of the human judgment applied to AI output is the thing you're actually paying for.

**Does Ze Lu use AI in his consulting work?**

Yes — extensively, for research, synthesis, drafting, and modeling. Every deliverable goes through multiple rounds of personal review, challenge, and editing before it reaches a client. The AI handles the mechanical parts; the judgment, the tradeoffs, and the ownership of the recommendations are mine.

**How can I avoid paying for sloppypasta?**

Ask the questions outlined above: have the consultant walk you through their reasoning on a past engagement, ask what they were uncertain about, ask who reviewed the work and what changed. Also pay attention to length — genuine thinking produces shorter, more actionable deliverables than AI-generated volume.
